<section style="margin-bottom:20px;">
<h3 style="background:#000;color:#fff;padding:6px 10px;display:inline-block;font-size:0.95rem;">
1. Scale Economics
</h3>

<p>
Scaling production to 10,000 units enables transition from prototype fabrication to industrial manufacturing efficiencies.
</p>

<p><strong>Manufacturing Effects at Scale</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Vertical component sourcing:</strong> direct procurement reduces per-unit cost via volume contracts</li>
<li><strong>CNC automation:</strong> enclosure production transitions to automated flat-pack workflows</li>
<li><strong>Process standardization:</strong> assembly becomes repeatable with reduced variance in tuning and calibration</li>
</ul>

<p>
At scale, the system architecture becomes a candidate for OEM integration depending on validation and manufacturing compatibility.
</p>
</section>

<section style="margin-bottom:20px;">
<h3 style="background:#000;color:#fff;padding:6px 10px;display:inline-block;font-size:0.95rem;">
4. Deployment Strategy
</h3>

<p><strong>Phase 1 — 1,000 units</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Validation of assembly pipeline</li>
<li>Field testing across use cases</li>
<li>Iterative refinement of enclosure and amplification</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Phase 2 — 5,000 units</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Supply chain optimization</li>
<li>Standardized assembly and calibration</li>
<li>OEM engagement begins</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Phase 3 — 10,000 units</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Production stabilization</li>
<li>Established distribution channels</li>
<li>Third-party integration potential</li>
</ul>
</section>

<section style="margin-bottom:20px;">

6. Risk and Constraints
